From Forest to Framework

Project
Year2024–25
DiplomaYear 5 — Diploma 13
LocationFairfields, Thetford, Norfolk, UK
ThemeTimber · Continuous Cover Forestry · Retrofit Housing

In this project I explore the localized application and distribution of timber from Continuous Cover Forestry to retrofit houses in Thetford town. Historically, the materials that built our homes have been local, visible and recognizable; today we live in homes disconnected from their material origin. My aim is to re-establish this connection, through Thetford Forest — an example of a plantation taking steps towards mixed species stands — and Thetford town, a typical post-war English settlement in close proximity.

In the early 19th century, London was a city on the rise. This period, fuelled by the Industrial Revolution, brought an explosion of urban growth and housing demand. The terraced house was born — a design tailored to meet the needs of London's growing middle class. The coal hole became a metaphor for spatial and social hierarchies; the separation between source and comfort mirrored in the construction of homes, where occupiers remain unaware of the origin of materials that make the home.

The Industrial Revolution marked a turning point for forestry. Monoculture plantations were designed to maximise efficiency but came with significant environmental costs. Today, Continuous Cover Forestry (CCF) maintains a continuous canopy, allows natural regeneration, and evolves into a multi-layered, multi-generational system. Trees grown under CCF develop irregular forms: knots, forks, tapered stems, curved grain — unusable by automated sawmill systems, yet structurally optimised. Every branch, curve and fork is a rational response to wind, light and gravity.

I look at bringing the forest back into the house. Through the frequent remodelling of homes at Fairfield I see vast opportunity for on-site interventions: a broken banister replaced with a juvenile trunk; a window sill from a curved offcut that becomes a seat or desk; a wall opening aided by a tree fork shaped around the ceiling beam. Homes can be materially and structurally shaped by the forest — and in turn, create a demand for timber that supports the expansion of CCF in Thetford.
